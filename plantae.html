Plantae, a plant care mobile app.

Four group members and I collaborated together to create a plant care mobile app that covered daily plant care tasks, an information database, and community platform to connect with other plant parents.

  • My Role: UX Designer, UX Researcher (Group Project)
  • Collaborators: Marissa Patterson, Morgan Smith, Kristen Talbot, Bao Tran
  • Timeframe: 3 weeks
  • Tools: Figma, Miro, Whimsical, Zoom
  • Deliverable: Mid-fidelity Mobile Prototype

User Research focuses on placing people at the center of the design process so that we can design things that are truly relevant to users by applying qualitative and quantitative methodologies.

User Research

  • Creating Surveys
  • Affinity Diagram

Our team brainstormed together on who would be a potential user for our plant care application. This was the Proto Persona (pictured to the right above). We came together and reflected upon our own plant parent misgivings and issues and ultimately came up with a user who wants to successfully grow more plants but also has a busy schedule to keep in mind.

My team and I proceeded to go down the list of items that we needed to check off our to-do list and created an online checklist so that we all could keep track of which person was at which stage of their divied up tasks.

Our Research Plan was formed after we discussed at length about which features we wante to focus on, our target audience, and the general need for such an application to improve the user experience. We proceeded to create an Interview Plan detailing what questions we would ask during interviews and discussed together at length about which questions might provide us with the best insight and information gathering.


My teammate, Marissa, conducted 5 user interviews with 15 questions prepared. They ranged from questions pertaining to their interest in plants, their general plant care experience, and how they determine if they are going to look at plants in general. We also wrote out the interview transcripts and recorded all the interviews so that we could look back on their answers later on so that we didn't mishear anything.


    


After interviews were finished, I started creating an Affinity Diagram (pictured above). I took the interview answers, typed them onto sticky notes on a Miro board, and grouped them together based on the categories or similarities of the sticky notes' contents.

This information was when used to create an updated User Persona (pictured below) based off actual data and not our original assumptions of a target user of this application.

Going off the type of interview questions we used, I went on to gather additional data by creating a survey on Google Forms to hand out.

Surprisingly, 43.5% of participants (10 out of 23 people) said they owned more than 7 plants which surprised our team. We had assumed that most participants would range between 1 to 3 but to see that owning plants was such a common practice was also reassuring for creating our mobile application.

The define and ideate phase is so we can analyze data, define users, and try to figure out what the users' potential problems may be. When we ideate, we think about generating new ideas.

 

Ideation


  • Problem Statement
  • User Insight Statement
  • Brainstorming
  • Feature Prioritization Matrix
  • User Scenario
  • Storyboard
  • User Journey Map

Next up was identifying what potential problems or issues that travelers might have with their travel planning process. My team and I developed the following Problem Statement:

  • “We believe that providing regular reminders and notifications for plant care specific to the species will result in plant owners being equipped with knowledge for how to ensure a long and healthy life for their plants.”


After we narrowed down the main issue facing plant parents, we developed the following User Insight Statement. This summarized the problem statement along with data gathered through interviews.

  • “Eager plant enthusiasts need ways to receive reminders for soil changes, watering, and sunlight because a lack of routine on plant care has decreased plant life and health.

    During the survey, we discovered that a large portion/81.8% of participants actually did not have a dedicated plant care schedule.

    Therefore, we believe that providing them with an application that provides consistent plant care reminders will increase plant longevity and health."

After identifying the "problems", we followed up with Brainstorming (pictured above to the right). We thought up what features we would like to see in the application and split them into I Like, I Wish, and What If sections. I Like stood for viable features, I Wish stood for features that could happen but might take a bit more effort, and What If for absolutely zany ideas.


We narrowed down my list of ideas from the brainstorming session and used to Now Wow! How? method to create a Feature Prioritization Matrix in Miro. We thought about what features we definitely needed (time management) and what might not be so important at the moment (calculating expenses over a period of time or needing separate categories).


I followed up and created a User Scenario (pictured to the right) based on our interview data, user persona, and user insight statement. I made sure to include as much detail as I could so that I could interpret a busy plant parents' life as accurately as possible when events or issues come up.

Next, my teammate Bao created a Storyboard (pictured below) based off what I wrote in my User Scenario. I helped add pictures and used free-for-use vectors from Freepik. I utilized a variety of pictures to showcase how our user's life would be if they introduced a plant into their life thanks to the Plantae application.





Lastly, we created a User Journey Map to show how Valeria, our user, would go through a "rollercoaster of emotions," so to speak, when trying to obtain and take care of a plant. It is a living being in the end and a lot of effort to take care of.

The journey map allows us to empathize further with how she might be feeling or gives me an idea of potential things to add or take away from the design process. Next, we went onto sketching and prototyping.

Prototyping simulates how finished product will work and helps us think through what potential designs may work. It is so we can test products and ideas before sinking a lot of time into the final product.

 

Prototyping


  • Competior Analysis
  • User Flow
  • Wireframe Sketches
  • Low-fi Digital Wireframing
  • Clickable Prototyping




We created a Competitor Analysis (pictured above to the right) and together we looked up various other plant care applications and weighed out the pros and cons of each one. One major factor in our deecision-making was that key features were considered "premium" and required payment on these applications. A lot of them offered watering reminders for free but other information was paywalled.

Next, I developed a User Flow (pictured below) based on the features we picked. I learned a lot about creating user flows when creating this one compared to my first solo application case study. I went back and forth with asking my teammates for their thoughts about the potential user flow.




Based on my user flow, we all went on to do some Sketches (pictured above). This is where our differences as a team came out the most. We all realized at the same time that we had different visions about how things would look on our mobile app beyond the onboarding screens. Some of us wanted to have plants displayed on the front home page, some of us didn't see the need to include all that information on the home page, some of us preferred tabs over buttons, etc. This was the most time-consuming part of our collaboration: sketching and low-fidelity designing. We were all trying to consolidate our ideas to create a singular product.

              

After figuring out the general idea of the designs we were going for, we designed our Low-Fidelity Prototype (pictured above) using Whimsical. We were all in a Zoom call together as we talked about how to consolidate all of our ideas into one vision. After we designed these, we moved onto doing testing. Testing is an important way for designers to have unbiased users look over a product or idea and give their honest feedback. Sometimes working on something for a long period of time will skew one's opinion so it is always important to get feedback for usability or confusing wording.

 

Testing


  • Usability Testing Plan
  • Usability Test Analysis
  • Iterated User Flow
  • Mid-fidelity Clickable
    Prototype

After finishing designing our low-fidelity prototype, we moved onto the testing phase. We performed Usability Testing on 5 participants. The tests were done over Zoom. The testers remote controled the Figma screen to go through the tasks on our prototype.

Tasks:
  • Sign up for an account on Plantae.
  • Complete the survey to find plant recommendations.
  • Add a plant from the survey results to your wishlist.
  • Add a plant to your plant list via the camera.
  • Create a community post.

Through our testing, we found out that we needed to simplify the plant information on the plant overview pages, specifically on our plant survey result page. We had overloaded it with too much information and made the choosing process clunky. We also needed to reword a lot of parts for clarity and be mindful of the icons we chose for certain parts of our app as to not confuse people.











After usability testing, I went back to my old user flow and iterated on it based on my added actions and feedback. The Iterated User Flow (pictured above) improved my understanding of the processes and was discussed at length with my team.

Finally, it was time to add color, utilize feedback, and apply our knowledge to the Mid-Fidelity Wireframes (pictured below). I took into account our bright but muted green focal color and applied that around the mid-fidelity prototype. We made all the buttons the same rounded design, added color for "cancel" and "stop" related buttons as red, and also added pictures and iOS design principles to our final product. Final Thoughts


Working as a team:

This was our first group project together so it was a learning process to remember to talk to our teammates as we went through design ideas. We couldn't just apply a color or design without first consulting with another team member. We also didn't want to step on each other's toes or nag another about their work so creating a task list on Trello really helped to see who was doing what work.
Lots of iteration:

I realized that iterating on a design was important after iterating on the user flow for the 5th time in a row. I put a lot of thought into how the user would navigate the process on the application. This only increased after designing how the plant care application would look like in terms of onboarding to identifying a plant to adding one. We went through at least 3-4 different designs.
For the future?

We would definitely add more color into the application aside from just greens and also enhance the community feature, including the ability to add topic tags to post, filter for topics, and follow topics and users. We also want to add an ability to directly connect with plant doctors or specialists as a premium feature. A more easily accessible feature to add would be a "plant shop map" that would give you a list of plant shops near you.
